Introduction About Electric Vehicles

Electric vehicles (EVs) are vehicles that are powered by electric motors and rechargeable battery packs, unlike traditional vehicles that use internal combustion engines fueled by gasoline or diesel.

Learn More

Benefits of EVs

Environmental Benefits

EVs produce zero tailpipe emissions, reducing air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions. They also contribute to reducing dependence on fossil fuels.

ECONOMIC: Most of the electricity used is produced locally, which creates and sustains jobs. Local manufacturing can benefit from EV purchases as numerous electric vehicles and EV parts are produced throughout Duke Energy’s territory. EV drivers also spend less on fuel so they have more funds to spend on other local goods.

ENVIRONMENTALEVs have no direct emissions and they are significantly cleaner than combustion engine vehicles. They average 75 miles per gallon equivalent including emissions from electricity production in India, while traditional combustion engine cars average less than 15 kilometers per liter of petrol. These metrics have steadily improved over the years and will continue to improve as the power sector in India shifts towards renewable energy sources and reduces the emissions intensity of generation resources.

Economic Benefits

EVs have lower operating costs compared to gasoline vehicles due to cheaper electricity rates and fewer moving parts. They are also eligible for tax incentives and rebates.

PRICE:EV and hybrid EV models are competitively priced at $37,000 and less, including popular models like the Chevrolet Bolt®, Ford Fusion Energi®, Honda Clarity®, and Mitsubishi Outlander®. Models at $30,000 or less include the Nissan LEAF® and Toyota Prius Prime®. Additionally, there's a growing market for used electric cars, which typically cost less than new models.

FUEL: Electric cars offer significant savings on fuel costs compared to traditional vehicles. On average, an electric car can save around $1,000 in fuel each year. The cost to fully charge a 60-kilowatt-hour (kWh) EV with more than 230 miles of range at home is typically less than $8. Moreover, energy prices are generally more stable compared to oil prices, providing consistency in budgeting for EV owners.

MAINTENANCE:The maintenance cost of electric cars, especially battery-powered models, is generally much lower than fossil fuel-powered vehicles due to fewer moving parts. EVs typically don't require oil changes and have single-speed transmissions, reducing maintenance expenses. Additionally, many EV battery packs come with warranties of at least eight years or 100,000 miles, with minimal degradation in power capacity over time.

INCENTIVES:Various countries offer incentives for purchasing electric vehicles, including tax credits and subsidies. In the United States, for example, there's a federal tax credit of up to $7,500 for buying a new EV. These incentives aim to promote the adoption of electric vehicles and reduce emissions.

Performance Benefits

EVs offer instant torque, providing smooth and quiet acceleration. They also have lower center of gravity due to battery placement, improving handling and stability.

PERFORMANCE:Electric vehicles offer excellent performance, with quick acceleration and high torque delivery. Numerous EVs can go from zero to 60 mph in 6 seconds or less, making them suitable for various driving conditions. Additionally, the regenerative braking technology enhances efficiency and contributes to a sporty driving experience

COMFORT: EVs provide a quieter and more comfortable ride compared to combustion engine vehicles, thanks to the absence of engine noise and vibrations. The smooth ride, combined with advanced suspension systems, enhances the overall comfort for passengers.

CONVENIENCE: EVs offer the convenience of charging at home, work, or public charging stations. With increasing charging infrastructure, EV owners have the flexibility to charge their vehicles wherever they go. Many workplaces also provide EV charging as an amenity, further enhancing convenience for owners.

PRACTICALITY: Electric vehicles are practical for daily use, offering ample storage space and charging options. They can be charged at home, work, or on the road, providing flexibility for owners. Additionally, advancements in battery technology are extending the driving range of EVs, making them suitable for long-distance travel.

SAFETY:Electric vehicles are designed with safety in mind, featuring advanced safety systems and crash protection measures. Many EV models have received top safety ratings from regulatory agencies, ensuring peace of mind for drivers and passengers.

Types of Electric Vehicles

Battery Electric Vehicles (BEVs)

BEVs are fully electric vehicles powered solely by battery packs. They do not have an internal combustion engine and produce zero emissions.

BEVs are also known as All-Electric Vehicles (AEV). Electric Vehicles using BEV technology run entirely on a battery-powered electric drivetrain. The electricity used to drive the vehicle is stored in a large battery pack which can be charged by plugging into the electricity grid. The charged battery pack then provides power to one or more electric motors to run the electric car.

Working Principles of BEV: The power for the electric motor is converted from the DC Battery to AC. As the accelerator is pressed, a signal is sent to the controller. The controller adjusts the speed of the vehicle by changing the frequency of the AC power from the inverter to the motor. The motor then connects and leads to the turning of wheels through a cog. If the brakes are pressed, or the electric car is decelerating, the motor becomes an alternator and produces power, which is sent back to the battery

Plug-in Hybrid Electric Vehicles (PHEVs)

PHEVs have both an electric motor and an internal combustion engine. They can run on electricity from the battery or gasoline, offering flexibility and extended range.

The PHEVs are also known as series hybrids. They have both engine and a motor. You can choose among the fuels, conventional fuel (such as petrol) or alternative fuel (such as bio-diesel). It can also be powered by a rechargeable battery pack. The battery can be charged externally.

PHEVs can run in at least 2 modes:
All-electric Mode, in which the motor and battery provide all the car’s energy Hybrid Mode, in which both electricity and petrol/diesel are employed

Working Principles of PHEV:PHEVs start-up in all-electric mode and make use of electricity until their battery pack is depleted. Once the battery gets drained, the engine takes over, and the vehicle operates as a conventional, non-plug-in hybrid. PHEVs can be charged by plugging into an outside electric power source, engine, or regenerative braking. When brakes are applied, the electric motor acts as a generator, using the energy to charge the battery. The engine’s power is supplemented by the electric motor; as a result, smaller engines can be used, increasing the car’s fuel efficiency without compromising performance.

Hybrid Electric Vehicles (HEVs)

HEVs combine an internal combustion engine with an electric motor and a small battery. They use regenerative braking to charge the battery and improve fuel efficiency.

HEVs are also known as series hybrid or parallel hybrid. HEVs have both engine and electric motor. The engine gets energy from fuel, and the motor gets electricity from batteries. The transmission is rotated simultaneously by both engine and electric motor. This then drives the wheels.

Working Principles of HEV:The fuel tank supplies energy to the engine like a regular car. The batteries run on an electric motor. Both the engine and electric motor can turn the transmission at the same time.
